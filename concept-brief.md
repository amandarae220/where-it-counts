# Where It Counts
### Concept Brief — Working Draft

---

"My vote doesn't matter."

It's one of the most common reasons Americans give for not voting. And here's the uncomfortable truth: they're not entirely wrong. Not because their vote doesn't count — it does. But because of *where they live*, the outcome was probably already decided before they walked into a polling booth.

That's not apathy talking. That's math.

---

## How the Lines Got Drawn

For forty years, politicians have been drawing their own district maps. After every Census, the party in power in each state redraws the lines — congressional districts, state legislative districts, sometimes local ones too. The goal isn't fair representation. The goal is to stay in power. They pack their opponents into as few districts as possible, spread their own voters across as many as they can win, and lock in outcomes that protect their careers regardless of how voters feel.

It's the equivalent of getting hired for a job and then rewriting the job description so your boss can't fire you. Except we the people are the boss — and we've been slowly, legally, quietly shut out.

That's gerrymandering. And it's worked.

But gerrymandering didn't create the problem from scratch — it amplified one that was already happening. Over decades, Americans have naturally clustered with people who think like them. Cities got more liberal. Rural areas got more conservative. Not because of any grand plan, but because of jobs, cost of living, culture, proximity to family. The sorting happened organically. Gerrymandering looked at that sorting and drew it into law. Choosing where to live by choice? Great. Feeling locked into where you live? Not so great.

The result: Americans are more divided than we've ever been. We live in cultural and political silos. While social media has made it easier than ever to find your people, at the end of the day, it is still a business. That's the business model. The sorting that gerrymandering drew into law, social media turned into a daily experience. Most Americans now live somewhere their vote was already decided before Election Day, surrounded by people who all got the same news feed.

## What the Data Actually Shows

The Electoral College — the system everyone loves to argue about — was designed for a geographically *distributed* country. It rewards states, not pile-ups. So when a city generates hundreds of thousands more votes than a state needs to be called, those extra votes don't go anywhere. They don't transfer. They don't help. They just disappear into a number that was already big enough.

New York City alone generated a net margin of 1.3 million votes over Trump in 2020 — while New York State only needed about 1 million total to be called. LA County added another **897,000** surplus votes on top of that. Cook County (Chicago): **565,000** more. Across the country in 2020, **76 million votes** — nearly half of all votes cast — were cast in places where the outcome was already set. Either they piled up past the point of influence, or they went to a candidate who lost. Seventy-six million votes that showed up, got counted, and didn't move the needle. Because of geography.

Meanwhile, Georgia was decided by **11,779 votes**. Arizona by **10,457**. Wisconsin by **20,682**. Georgia, Arizona, and Wisconsin combined — three states worth a combined 37 electoral votes — were decided by fewer than **43,000 votes total**. That's less than a sold-out football stadium.

California's surplus alone could have flipped Georgia **216 times over**.

The blue-state surplus was **146 times** the combined margin in those three states. Not 10 times. Not 20 times. A hundred and forty-six.

And state legislative races? Even smaller. Individual districts inside those same counties were decided by a few hundred votes — the size of one apartment building. Some county-level races in 2020 were decided by fewer than 20 votes. Inyo County, California: **14 votes**. Ontario County, New York: **14 votes**. Not 14 percentage points. Fourteen people. State legislatures control Medicaid expansion, education funding, what goes on your ballot, and who draws the next round of district maps in 2031. The most consequential political decisions in most Americans' daily lives happen at the state and local level. And those are the races decided by the smallest numbers.

Here's the part that doesn't get said enough: this problem hits one side significantly harder than the other — and not for the reasons you might think. County by county across the entire country, Republican surplus votes totaled **8.6 million** in 2020. Democratic surplus votes totaled **11.8 million**. That gap — 3.2 million more surplus Democratic votes than Republican — isn't because Democrats are less strategic. It's because blue counties have bigger, denser cities that pile up votes faster. LA County's surplus alone (**897,000**) is larger than the total surplus of the ten most lopsided red counties in America combined. The geographic concentration problem is structurally worse for Democrats — which is exactly why this conversation matters more urgently on that side, and why the people most capable of changing it are sitting in apartments they can't afford, in cities where their vote doesn't move anything.

## The Part Nobody's Talking About

A lot of people who live in major cities are originally from somewhere else. They moved for opportunity, for culture, for connection — and many are still there not because it's affordable, but because they're afraid of what leaving means. They don't want to be the only person in the room who thinks the way they do. They've written off entire states, entire regions, as places they couldn't survive.

And then there's the house.

"I can't afford to buy a home" has become the defining experience for millions of city dwellers — especially those in their 30s who did everything right, have good jobs, and still can't make the math work. That's not separate from the political story. It *is* the political story. The same geographic concentration that made your vote not count also drove housing costs to the point of exclusion. The sorting happened politically and economically at the same time, through the same forces. The city that promised opportunity delivered it — and then priced out the people who earned it.

There's another layer that almost nobody will say out loud.

A lot of the people most capable of changing the communities they grew up in left those communities — and then slowly stopped feeling like they could go back. They went off to college, absorbed a set of values, built a new identity, and somewhere along the way started treating home like something to have escaped from rather than something to return to. That's not an accident. That's a cultural failure — and Democrats own a significant share of it.

Progressive culture allowed a quiet classism to take hold. One that treats leaving your hometown as graduation and staying as settling. That signals to people that ambition looks like a certain zip code, a certain career, a certain social circle. That serious people live in cities. That rural and small-town America is a place you come *from*, not a place you choose. The communities on the receiving end of that attitude noticed. They felt the condescension long before anyone said it out loud. And a lot of them stopped voting for the party that seemed to look down on them — not because their values changed, but because they felt they'd been written off.

The intellectual flight is real. Bright, capable people left. The communities that needed local doctors, teachers, lawyers, entrepreneurs, and civic leaders watched them go. And those same people are now sitting in expensive apartments in cities they can barely afford, feeling stuck — not because they want to stay, but because they've been told, in a thousand small ways, that going home means giving up.

And somewhere in all of this, we lost the in-between.

The same way the middle class got hollowed out — squeezed from both sides until it stopped being a realistic place to land — the political middle got hollowed out too. City Republicans. Country leftists. People who grew up hunting and also believe in universal healthcare. People who live in a blue city and don't share the politics of their block. They exist. They've always existed. But the geographic sorting, the social media algorithms, the gerrymandered maps, and the cultural messaging from both sides have made them invisible — or made them feel like they have to pick a box and stay in it.

We turned geography into identity. If you're from rural Tennessee, you're supposed to vote one way. If you live in Portland, another. The stereotype became the expectation. The expectation became the pressure. And the pressure drove out anyone who didn't fit cleanly into either corner — not just politically, but literally. We're not sorting people by where they live anymore. We're telling them who they're allowed to be based on their zip code.

That's the fear. And it's a lie.

The data on what's actually happening inside so-called red states tells a very different story than the label on the outside. Suburbs that were reliably one-sided a decade ago are genuinely competitive today. Communities of like-minded people exist in every state — they're just not concentrated enough yet to show up in the headline numbers.

And increasingly, many of these same people can work from anywhere.

The cost-of-living math is stark. The median rent in New York City is **$3,406 a month**. In Columbus, Ohio — a genuinely competitive metro — it's **$1,515**. That's **$1,891 a month in savings. Nearly $23,000 a year.** Enough to build a down payment in three years on a house that costs $330,000 in Columbus and would cost $718,000 in New York. San Francisco to Milwaukee: $1,666 a month saved, $20,000 a year. Boston to Raleigh: $1,509 a month, $18,000 a year. Home ownership — the thing that was supposed to come after doing everything right — becomes realistic again. And the civic weight of that move? Enormous.

## The Long Game

The 2030 Census is four years away. After it, every district in the country gets redrawn. Those maps will determine who holds power for the decade that follows — through school board elections, state houses, congressional delegations, and redistricting cycles to come.

You can't gerrymander a neighborhood that has genuinely changed. If enough people have moved in, the underlying population forces the lines to shift. That's not a loophole — that's how it's supposed to work.

And beyond the math, there's something harder to quantify but just as real: when people who vote differently actually live near each other, the hatred that comes from never having met goes down. Study after study confirms it. The geographic sorting of the last forty years didn't just change election outcomes — it eliminated the daily contact between people with different views that democracy has always depended on. Moving doesn't fix that overnight. But it starts it.

## The Piece

*Where It Counts* is a scrolling editorial — data journalism in the tradition of The Pudding, NYT Upshot, and 538's long reads. It takes readers through the full argument: the history of geographic sorting and gerrymandering, the math of surplus votes at every level of the ballot, the cost-of-living case for moving, the data that dismantles the isolation fear, and the 2030 horizon that makes all of it actionable now.

Interactive maps show where votes pile up uselessly and where they'd change something. A cost-of-living comparison shows what the same money gets you in competitive areas. State legislative and school board margins show how small the numbers really are. And a secondary tool lets readers find competitive areas that match their income, job type, and cost-of-living needs.

It is not a call to pick a side. It is a case — built on public data, grounded in civics — for Americans to think differently about the relationship between where they live and how much their voice actually matters.
